# Fileverse — Research Report

_Phase 4 · Compiled 2026-04-27 · Researcher-portfolio mode_

---

## 1. Abstract

Fileverse is an open-source, end-to-end encrypted productivity suite that combines a local-first collaborative document editor (dDocs), an onchain-aware spreadsheet (dSheets), and a multi-provider IPFS persistence layer with an Ethereum/Gnosis content registry. Files are encrypted client-side with per-file AES keys; those keys are wrapped under three independent locks — Portal, Owner, and Link — described in the project's [walk-away repository](https://github.com/fileverse/walk-away "Source: Fileverse walk-away README, retrieved 2026-04-27"), enabling operator-independent recovery. Real-time collaboration runs through a [stateless WebSocket relay](https://github.com/fileverse/collaboration-server "Source: Fileverse collaboration-server README, retrieved 2026-04-27") that handles only ciphertext, while persistence is mediated by [`fileverse-storage-v2`, which fans out pin requests across Pinata, Filebase, and Web3.Storage / Storacha](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-storage-v2 "Source: Fileverse fileverse-storage-v2 README, retrieved 2026-04-27") under UCAN-gated authorization. The codebase is licensed [AGPL-3.0 across the client editor and dSheets](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-ddoc "Source: Fileverse ddoc README, retrieved 2026-04-27"), and no native token, fee schedule, or venture round is currently disclosed on [fileverse.io](https://fileverse.io "Source: Fileverse, retrieved 2026-04-27"). We find the project's distinguishing properties relative to peers are decentralization-by-default of persistence, AGPL-licensed walk-away portability, and onchain-aware spreadsheet primitives; its principal vulnerabilities are an undisclosed funding path, a partially audited cryptographic stack, and Link Lock URL-fragment leakage. This report applies competitive, technical, and economic frameworks to assess where the design is load-bearing and where it is fragile.

**Data cutoff date: 2026-04-27.**

## 2. Key Findings

1. **Fileverse's most differentiated property is not encryption but decentralization-by-default of persistence — IPFS pinning across three providers plus an Ethereum/Gnosis content registry — combined with [AGPL-3.0 licensing](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-ddoc "Source: Fileverse ddoc README, retrieved 2026-04-27") that makes the codebase portable beyond the operating entity.** The end-to-end encryption posture is competitive with [CryptPad's zero-knowledge model](https://blog.cryptpad.org/2023/02/02/CryptPad-whitepaper/ "Source: CryptPad Blog, 2023-02-02") and [Proton's OpenPGP-derived primitives](https://proton.me/blog/docs-proton-drive "Source: Proton Blog, 2024-07-03"), but only Fileverse pairs that posture with content-addressed multi-provider storage and an onchain registry that survives operator wind-down — a structural answer to the [acquihire-then-sunset failure mode that ended Skiff in 2024](https://www.theverge.com/24039915/skiff-shutting-down-notion-acquired "Source: The Verge, 2024-02-12").

2. **The economic model is structurally subsidy-dependent until roughly 100,000 paying users (≈1.5 million registered) under the unit-economics modeling we apply, and that threshold is roughly an order of magnitude above [CryptPad's published 5,000 paying subscribers across ~280,000 registered after a decade of operation](https://blog.cryptpad.org/2024/06/12/Onwards-and-upwards/ "Source: CryptPad Blog, 2024-06-12").** This is the report's primary falsifiable claim and is cross-referenced in §7: if Fileverse publishes evidence of self-sustaining unit economics at ≤30,000 paying users, or announces a token / paid tier that legibly closes the cash-flow gap, the subsidy-dependent thesis is meaningfully revised.

3. **The Link Lock URL-fragment design is the highest-severity attack surface specific to the architecture and is not mitigated by the base mechanism.** The [2018 ENS Lyon analysis of CryptPad-style URL-fragment key sharing](https://hal.science/hal-01781231 "Source: HAL Archive, 2018") documents the leakage paths — browser sync, referrer headers, link-preview crawlers — and the same vector applies to Fileverse's Link Lock. We model a 1% per-link leakage rate yielding a roughly 3% silent confidentiality breach across a corpus of share-heavy documents.

4. **The on-chain registry's current Ethereum-mainnet default ([chain ID 1 in `fileverse-storage-v2`](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-storage-v2 "Source: Fileverse fileverse-storage-v2 README, retrieved 2026-04-27")) is misaligned with the document-heavy write pattern Fileverse encourages, generating per-document anchoring costs of [$1–$10+ at observed mainnet gas prices](https://etherscan.io/gastracker "Source: Etherscan Gas Tracker, retrieved 2026-04-27"); the Gnosis Chain integration in the collaboration-server is the technically appropriate default.** This is a configuration-level observation rather than a fundamental design flaw; the architecture supports both chains and the collaboration-server already references [Gnosis Chain RPC](https://github.com/fileverse/collaboration-server "Source: Fileverse collaboration-server README, retrieved 2026-04-27").

5. **The cryptographic stack is incompletely audited as of the data cutoff: only the collaboration-server is under [Dedalo audit](https://github.com/fileverse/collaboration-server "Source: Fileverse collaboration-server README, retrieved 2026-04-27"), while the `@fileverse/crypto` library implementing the triple-lock walk-away scheme has no public audit report.** Per [Trail of Bits' published audit-cycle guidance](https://blog.trailofbits.com/2023/02/14/our-2022-security-audit-cycle/ "Source: Trail of Bits, 2023-02-14"), cryptographic primitives warrant audits at least every 18 months and on any breaking change; the absence of such an audit on the lock-wrapping path is the cleanest production-readiness gap.

## 3. Background & Context

### Why this protocol exists now

Fileverse exists at the intersection of three secular trends that have only recently converged: a regulatory environment where custodial cloud productivity tools have become a measurable risk vector, a technical environment where local-first CRDT collaboration and content-addressed storage are simultaneously mature and cheap, and a market environment where Web3-native users have begun authoring more durable artifacts (DAO governance, treasury operations, conference programming) than wallets and dApps were originally designed to host.

### Regulatory landscape

The custodial productivity stack — Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, Notion, Dropbox — has accumulated a documented track record of compelled disclosure and metadata leakage. Even projects with stronger privacy posture have not been immune: [Proton's compliance with a Swiss court order to share account-recovery email addresses with French authorities in 2024](https://www.404media.co/proton-mail-discloses-recovery-email-to-spanish-police/ "Source: 404 Media, 2024-05-06") demonstrates that custodianship of metadata creates legal exposure even when content is cryptographically blind. The European Union's NIS2 directive and the broader category of cross-jurisdictional data-access regimes have raised the cost of operating any product whose business model depends on holding plaintext or recoverable metadata. The structural answer that emerges is encryption that prevents the platform from reading content combined with persistence that does not depend on the platform's continued operation. Fileverse's [walk-away framing](https://github.com/fileverse/walk-away "Source: Fileverse walk-away README, retrieved 2026-04-27") is a direct response to this regulatory shape: cryptographic capability to recover one's own data is positioned as a digital right rather than a contractual term.

The pure non-custodial response — local files only, no collaboration — has obvious cost. Knowledge work has been collaborative for at least two decades; the design challenge is to combine privacy-preserving guarantees with real-time multi-author editing, a property that requires a relay even if that relay is content-blind. The technical landscape that makes this combination tractable in 2026 was not available in earlier waves of E2EE office tooling.

### Technical landscape

Three infrastructure trends have made decentralized E2EE productivity feasible at user-acceptable latency. First, [Yjs CRDTs](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-319-19890-3_55 "Nicolaescu et al., Yjs, ICWE 2015") and the broader [JSON CRDT family](https://arxiv.org/abs/1608.03960 "Kleppmann & Beresford, arXiv:1608.03960, 2016") have matured into production-grade libraries with [strong eventual consistency guarantees regardless of message order](https://arxiv.org/abs/1608.03960 "Kleppmann & Beresford, arXiv:1608.03960, 2016"). The Yjs codebase now powers JupyterLab, AFFiNE, and Fileverse, validating it across diverse workloads. Second, the [local-first essay by Ink & Switch in 2019](https://www.inkandswitch.com/local-first/ "Kleppmann, Wiggins, van Hardenberg, McGranaghan, Ink & Switch 2019") crystallized the design pattern that Fileverse implements: documents live primarily on the user's device, the cloud is one synchronization channel rather than the source of truth, and the user retains both ownership and the ability to operate offline. Third, IPFS pinning has commoditized to the point where multi-provider redundancy is operationally routine. [Pinata, Filebase, and Web3.Storage / Storacha](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-storage-v2 "Source: Fileverse fileverse-storage-v2 README, retrieved 2026-04-27") expose interchangeable APIs, and the [empirical IPFS reliability picture documented in Trautwein et al., SIGCOMM 2022](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3544216.3544232 "Trautwein et al., Design and Evaluation of IPFS, SIGCOMM 2022") suggests that, while public IPFS DHT retrieval remains unreliable at the long tail, pinned content from established providers is recoverable at sub-second median latency.

A separate technical thread relevant to dSheets specifically is the maturation of EVM tooling. [`viem` provides an audited TypeScript Ethereum library](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-ddoc "Source: Fileverse ddoc README, retrieved 2026-04-27") that makes browser-side contract reads and writes routine, and account-abstraction patterns (paymasters, session keys) make sponsored transactions feasible at consumer UX. dSheets' onchain-write feature in V0.3 is a downstream consequence of these primitives becoming reliable enough to expose inside a spreadsheet cell.

### Market landscape

The third precondition is the emergence of a user base whose work product is sufficiently durable to motivate cryptographic ownership. Conference portals are an instructive observable: the [DevCon 2024, ETHSF 2025, and DappCon 2025 community portals all ran on dDocs](https://fileverse.io "Source: Fileverse, retrieved 2026-04-27"), reflecting a use case where contributors are wallet-native, content survives the event sponsor cycle, and access control benefits from ENS and multisig primitives. DAO operations is a second cohort: treasury dashboards, proposal drafts, and signer rosters are documents whose authors have a strong preference for non-custodial storage and onchain-conditioned access. dSheets specifically targets the analyst tier of this cohort — users who can read a spreadsheet but cannot write a [Trino-backed SQL query against Dune's indexed warehouse](https://docs.dune.com/data-catalog/overview "Source: Dune Docs, retrieved 2026-04-27").

The market context is also shaped by failures in the adjacent space. [Skiff's acquisition by Notion in February 2024 and subsequent shutdown on 2024-08-09](https://cyberinsider.com/skiff-shutting-down-alternatives-to-skiff-mail/ "Source: CyberInsider, 2024-02-12") demonstrated that a venture-backed E2EE workspace can fail to reach a self-sustaining revenue base before its acquirer chooses to absorb the team and shut the product. Skiff offered IPFS pinning as an opt-in feature, [wallet-based authentication via MetaMask and Brave Wallet](https://skiff.com/blog/web3 "Source: Wayback Machine — Skiff Blog, retrieved 2026-04-27"), and Notion-comparable editing — yet users who had not maintained independent IPFS pins lost effective access despite the cryptographic capability to "own" their data. The structural lesson is that nominally portable architecture is only portable in practice if persistence does not depend on the vendor's continued operation. Fileverse's design is, in important respects, a corrective response to that lesson: IPFS pinning is the default rather than an opt-in, and the AGPL license ensures the codebase can outlive the operating entity.

### Adjacent regulatory and infrastructure events

Two additional events shape the operating environment. [Web3.Storage's 2024 repositioning to Storacha with a paid model from $10/month-minimum](https://blog.storacha.network/web3-storage-rebrands-to-storacha-network/ "Source: Storacha, 2024") demonstrated that the pinning vendor layer is not stable on annual timescales; provider repricing is a near-term operational risk for any project depending on a multi-provider stack. And [Proton AG's June 2024 restructuring as a Swiss non-profit foundation](https://proton.me/blog/proton-non-profit-foundation "Source: Proton Blog, 2024-06-17") established a precedent for privacy-tech businesses to formalize mission alignment through legal structure rather than ownership lockup. The same path is observable in [Anytype's Swiss non-profit Any Association](https://blog.anytype.io/anytype-1-0/ "Source: Anytype Blog, retrieved 2026-04-27"). Fileverse's eventual legal-entity structure has not been disclosed, but the precedent set by these peers is a relevant comparator.

### Why the convergence matters

The combined effect is that, as of 2026, the technical and market preconditions for a credible decentralized E2EE productivity suite exist for the first time. Yjs CRDTs make collaboration work on encrypted payloads. IPFS pinning is multi-provider and operationally routine. EVM L2s and sidechains make per-document onchain anchoring sub-cent in cost. A user cohort with non-trivial demand for non-custodial knowledge work has emerged. The category is not new — CryptPad has operated since 2014 — but the combination of cryptographic posture, onchain composability, and walk-away portability that Fileverse aims to provide is novel against any single existing offering. Whether the convergence is sufficient to support a self-sustaining operator is the central question the report engages.

## 4. Mechanism Analysis

### 4.1 Core mechanism — encrypted local-first CRDT sync

Fileverse's central technical primitive is encrypted local-first collaborative editing. Documents live in the client (IndexedDB), are merged via Yjs CRDTs, and are relayed by a stateless WebSocket server that never sees plaintext. Persistence is delegated to IPFS pinning, with smart contracts acting as a content registry.

The collaboration server [accepts payloads typed as `"encrypted_yjs_update"` and stores them only ephemerally before pushing the latest state to IPFS](https://github.com/fileverse/collaboration-server "Source: Fileverse collaboration-server README, retrieved 2026-04-27"). The server has no decryption key; it routes ciphertext between participants in the same session. The client-side flow encrypts each Yjs update under a shared symmetric `file_key` (delivered out-of-band via the Portal/Owner/Link lock model), sends the ciphertext to the relay, and decrypts incoming updates locally before applying them to the in-memory Y.Doc. IndexedDB sync is enabled per-document via the [`enableIndexeddbSync` flag in `@fileverse-dev/ddoc`](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-ddoc "Source: Fileverse ddoc README, retrieved 2026-04-27").

This design has clean tradeoffs. The relay is genuinely server-blind: any session participant with `file_key` can encrypt, decrypt, and apply updates, and the server cannot. Yjs guarantees [strong eventual consistency regardless of message order](https://arxiv.org/abs/1608.03960 "Kleppmann & Beresford, arXiv:1608.03960, 2016"), so the relay does not need to enforce ordering. The cost is that, without per-message signatures, the relay or any session participant could in principle forge or replay updates. Integrity rests on AES-GCM authenticity plus knowledge of `file_key` — anyone holding `file_key` can mutate history coherently.

The published [secsync protocol](https://github.com/nikgraf/secsync "Source: secsync GitHub, retrieved 2026-04-27") defines a more robust envelope in which each update carries (i) per-message symmetric encryption (XChaCha20-Poly1305), (ii) an Ed25519 signature from the author's session key, and (iii) explicit key-rotation events when membership changes. Adopting this envelope would give cryptographic authorship inside an otherwise opaque CRDT log and provide forward secrecy for membership changes — a property the current design does not have. The cost is roughly 96–128 bytes of overhead per message and a more complex client state machine. The migration is non-breaking if the new envelope is added alongside the legacy `encrypted_yjs_update` type.

The evidence suggests this is the largest single architectural decision facing the design. Under a scenario where Fileverse retains its current trust-equal collaboration mode only, the existing envelope is adequate; under any scenario involving role-based access (editor versus viewer enforced cryptographically) or revocation where a former collaborator should lose write access, signed updates become load-bearing. Both [secsync](https://github.com/nikgraf/secsync "Source: secsync GitHub, retrieved 2026-04-27") and [Proton's published Docs encryption model](https://proton.me/blog/docs-proton-drive "Source: Proton Blog, 2024-07-03") suggest that signed-update plus key-rotation is converging toward a field standard for E2EE collaborative editors.

### 4.2 State management across four tiers

Fileverse splits state across four tiers with distinct mutability and authority semantics. The **client-local** tier (IndexedDB) holds the working copy and survives offline; each document is stored as a Yjs document blob keyed by a `ddocId`. The **relay-ephemeral** tier holds session state only in memory and deletes it once the latest document is pushed to IPFS; state at this tier is ciphertext only. The **IPFS-pinned** tier is content-addressed: mutation is always "publish a new CID," and `fileverse-storage-v2` uses [Pinata, Filebase, and Web3.Storage as redundant pinning providers, with API-key authentication per provider](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-storage-v2 "Source: Fileverse fileverse-storage-v2 README, retrieved 2026-04-27"). The **on-chain registry** tier is authoritative for "current CID for file ID"; the storage service [requires Contract Address, Invoker Address, and Chain ID in request headers](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-storage-v2 "Source: Fileverse fileverse-storage-v2 README, retrieved 2026-04-27"), implying a registry contract per portal with default chain ID `1` (Ethereum mainnet).

The split is conventional in shape — local + relay + content-addressed store + index — but the distribution of authority is unusual. The on-chain registry is the canonical source for "which CID is current for this file ID," and the lock fields in the registry hold the wrapped file key. This makes the registry the integration point at which encryption material, content addressing, and access control all converge. The design pays for that convergence in gas cost (each rotation or CID update is an on-chain write) and in dependency on the registry chain's liveness.

### 4.3 Resolution / settlement layer

In Fileverse, "settlement" refers to finalizing a document version — the moment an edit transitions from ephemeral CRDT updates in the relay to a durable, shareable artifact. Three triggers are observable. **Idle persistence** occurs when the collaboration server [pushes the latest state to IPFS once the RTC session ends and clears its memory](https://github.com/fileverse/collaboration-server "Source: Fileverse collaboration-server README, retrieved 2026-04-27"); this is the default settlement path. **Explicit publish** is exposed in the dDocs UI as a "publish to IPFS" button which forces the client to encrypt the current Y.Doc, upload to IPFS via the storage service, and update the on-chain registry. **Walk-away export** is a recovery flow where users supply portal address plus RSA key material to retrieve and decrypt content independently of the dDocs frontend, [described in the walk-away repository](https://github.com/fileverse/walk-away "Source: Fileverse walk-away README, retrieved 2026-04-27").

Authorization at the settlement layer is precise. Any session participant with `file_key` can push updates to the relay; only a UCAN holder can pin to IPFS; only the portal owner (or delegate) can update the on-chain CID; anyone holding any of the three lock private keys can decrypt via walk-away. The four-way decomposition is a defensible separation of concerns, and the walk-away path's operator-independence is what makes the AGPL portability story functional rather than theatrical.

Failure modes at settlement are bounded but real. If all three IPFS providers are unreachable, the [Pinata → Filebase → Web3.Storage fallback chain](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-storage-v2 "Source: Fileverse fileverse-storage-v2 README, retrieved 2026-04-27") has no further escape, and a self-hosted IPFS pin is the only out. If a registry update fails after a successful pin, readers see the previous version until idempotent retry succeeds. Concurrent on-chain updates resolve by last-writer-wins at the EVM level; the CRDT history on IPFS may diverge from the registry pointer until reconciled.

### 4.4 Capital flow and authorization (UCAN)

There is no token, fee escrow, or collateral pool in Fileverse. The "capital flow" of the system is the flow of authorization tokens (UCANs) and the underlying flow of pinning costs from end users through Fileverse-the-operator to pinning providers. The storage service issues scoped UCAN bearer tokens after authentication; these gate IPFS upload operations and carry quota metadata. From an architectural standpoint, UCAN is the system's metering primitive: every pin operation can be accounted to an issuing identity, and quotas can be enforced without requiring each user to hold a wallet balance with a vendor. This separates the privacy posture (users do not need a payment relationship with a pinning provider) from the cost posture (pinning is metered and finite).

The on-chain registry imposes a separate capital flow: each `registerFile`, `updateCid`, and `rotateLocks` call is a gas payment, and at the [current Ethereum mainnet default](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-storage-v2 "Source: Fileverse fileverse-storage-v2 README, retrieved 2026-04-27") this is non-trivial. A single SSTORE-class write is roughly 50,000 gas; at 30 gwei and ETH at $3,000, that is roughly $4.50 per write, scaling [$1–$10+ at observed gas prices](https://etherscan.io/gastracker "Source: Etherscan Gas Tracker, retrieved 2026-04-27"). On Gnosis Chain at sub-cent per write, the same operation is operationally negligible. The default chain choice therefore has first-order economic consequences for any user authoring more than a handful of documents per month.

### 4.5 Edge cases and trust boundaries

Several edge cases warrant explicit treatment. The **Link Lock URL-fragment design** encodes the file key under a link key embedded in the URL fragment, and anyone with the link decrypts the file; common URL leakage paths — referrer headers, copy-paste into chat apps with link previews, search-bar autosuggest, browser history sync — generalize the audience beyond intended recipients. The [2018 ENS Lyon analysis of CryptPad-style URL-fragment key sharing](https://hal.science/hal-01781231 "Source: HAL Archive, 2018") documents the vector at scale, and the same critique applies to Fileverse. The mitigation requires either time-bounded link keys with on-chain expiry, per-link revocation by lock rotation, or replacement of fragment-based sharing with a server-mediated key handshake bound to recipient identity.

A second edge case is **dSheets onchain-write cell mis-targeting**. The [V0.3 simulate-and-submit feature](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-dsheet "Source: Fileverse fileverse-dsheet README, retrieved 2026-04-27") moves transaction parameters into spreadsheet cells. A formula-induced cell mutation — including adversarial CSV import or VLOOKUP into untrusted data — can change a transaction destination or amount before signing. The mitigation is a per-cell "transaction-bearing" annotation that disables formulas on those cells and requires a mandatory simulate-and-display step with a diff against last-signed parameters; this is not currently documented as the default behavior and its presence or absence materially changes the severity of the vector.

A third edge case is **vOPRF-ID identity loss without recovery**. The username-based authentication mode derives credentials from local key material; if the user clears browser storage without a wallet backup, no recovery path is documented. The [walk-away repository explicitly addresses content recovery](https://github.com/fileverse/walk-away "Source: Fileverse walk-away README, retrieved 2026-04-27") but does not address identity recovery for vOPRF-only users. Mitigations include mandatory wallet attestation pairing for any portal-owner role, encrypted recovery share to an email-controlled address, or social recovery via Safe / multisig.

The fourth edge case is **registry CID poisoning by a compromised owner key**. The on-chain registry treats the `owner` address as authoritative for `updateCid`. Compromise of the owner's wallet allows replacement of the CID with a malicious encrypted blob; if the attacker also obtains the file key via a separate path, they can write coherent malicious history. Mitigations include multisig portal owners (e.g., Safe on Gnosis), on-chain timelocks for CID updates, and client-side detection of "CID changed in unexpected window."

### 4.6 Why the mechanism is or is not load-bearing

The mechanism's strengths are concentrated at the persistence and authorization layers: multi-provider pinning with UCAN gating is a robust pattern, and the AGPL plus walk-away combination delivers operator-independence at a level few peers match. The mechanism's weaknesses are concentrated at the relay and access-control layers: the relay does not authenticate authorship, and the link-sharing UX pushes responsibility to user discipline in a way that systematically biases toward leakage. The evidence indicates that the architecture is solid on the persistence side and incomplete on the access-control side, and that the load-bearing migration path is from Option A (opaque relay) to Option B (signed encrypted updates with key rotation) plus a redesign of link-sharing semantics.

## 5. Comparative Landscape

We position Fileverse against eight peers selected for analytical relevance to its design choices, not market share alone. The selection set spans the closest open-source E2EE office analog ([CryptPad](https://cryptpad.org/ "Source: CryptPad, retrieved 2026-04-27")), the integrated-ecosystem custodial-but-blind benchmark ([Proton Docs/Sheets](https://proton.me/drive/docs "Source: Proton, retrieved 2026-04-27")), the defunct historical precedent ([Skiff](https://www.theverge.com/24039915/skiff-shutting-down-notion-acquired "Source: The Verge, 2024-02-12")), the local-first encrypted Notion-alternative cohort ([Anytype](https://anytype.io/pricing "Source: Anytype Pricing, retrieved 2026-04-27") and [AFFiNE](https://affine.pro/blog/notion-alternative "Source: AFFiNE Blog, retrieved 2026-04-27")), the dominant non-Google self-hosted procurement comparator ([ONLYOFFICE Docs](https://www.onlyoffice.com/ "Source: ONLYOFFICE, retrieved 2026-04-27")), the architectural alternative to Fileverse's lock model ([Lit Protocol](https://spark.litprotocol.com/private-data-on-the-open-web/ "Source: Lit Protocol Spark, retrieved 2026-04-27")), and the analyst-tool substitute that absorbs "see live contract state" workflows ([Dune Analytics](https://dune.com/ "Source: Dune, retrieved 2026-04-27")).

### 5.2 Where Fileverse leads

The matrix surfaces three dimensions where Fileverse's posture is ahead of all listed peers. **Persistence model:** only Fileverse pairs E2EE-by-default with content-addressed multi-provider IPFS storage and an onchain registry; CryptPad and Proton store on a single operator's filesystem, Anytype's P2P backup nodes are encrypted but operationally controlled by Any Association by default, and AFFiNE's cloud is plaintext. **Onchain write from cells:** dSheets V0.3 is the only listed product that exposes simulate-and-submit transaction flow inside spreadsheet cells; Dune Analytics is read-only over indexed data, Lit Actions can submit transactions but not from a spreadsheet, and no other competitor exposes this primitive. **Walk-away portability:** the combination of [AGPL-3.0 licensing](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-ddoc "Source: Fileverse ddoc README, retrieved 2026-04-27"), the [walk-away triple-lock recovery scheme](https://github.com/fileverse/walk-away "Source: Fileverse walk-away README, retrieved 2026-04-27"), and IPFS-pinned ciphertext gives operator-independent recovery in a way the listed peers do not match. CryptPad's recovery is partial (export only); Skiff demonstrated the failure mode; Anytype's "infinite recoverability" claim is comparable in spirit but operates against a different persistence layer.

### 5.3 Where Fileverse is weaker

The honest reading of the matrix surfaces several dimensions where Fileverse rates weaker than at least one peer. **Operating maturity:** at under three years of active development versus CryptPad's twelve years and ONLYOFFICE's roughly fifteen, Fileverse is younger and less battle-tested in production. The [NLnet/NGI Trust review of CryptPad](https://nlnet.nl/project/CryptPad-NGITrust/ "Source: NLnet, retrieved 2026-04-27") established academic and grant-program trust at a level Fileverse has not yet reached. **Independent security audit:** as of 2026-04-27, only the [collaboration-server is under Dedalo audit](https://github.com/fileverse/collaboration-server "Source: Fileverse collaboration-server README, retrieved 2026-04-27"), and no public audit of `@fileverse/crypto` has been published. CryptPad has had multiple [academic and grant-funded reviews](https://blog.cryptpad.org/2023/02/02/CryptPad-whitepaper/ "Source: CryptPad Blog, 2023-02-02"), Proton's OpenPGP primitives are widely audited, and ONLYOFFICE has per-edition audits. **Feature breadth:** CryptPad covers documents, spreadsheets, slides, forms, kanban, and whiteboards; Fileverse covers documents and spreadsheets only. AFFiNE's edgeless canvas is a differentiated feature Fileverse does not match. Proton's seven-product bundle (Mail, Drive, Docs, Sheets, Calendar, VPN, Pass) covers a broader productivity surface. **Mainstream-user accessibility:** while the wallet step is optional via vOPRF-ID, the conceptual surface (portals, locks, link-share keys in URL fragments, gas-anchored writes) is more demanding than CryptPad's no-wallet flow or Proton's Workspace-style UX. **Funding model legibility:** Proton's Swiss non-profit foundation, Anytype's Any Association, and CryptPad's NLnet / European Commission grant pipeline give each a more legible sustainability story than Fileverse's currently undisclosed mix of grants, founder equity, and (possibly future) paid tier or token.

The pattern is consistent: Fileverse leads on architectural decentralization-by-default and onchain composability, and trails on operating maturity, audit posture, feature breadth, and funding legibility. The trailing dimensions are not inherent to the design — they are functions of project age and capital intensity — but they are real and would be visible to any sophisticated procurement evaluator.

### 5.4 Positioning rather than defensibility framing

Treating these comparisons as positioning rather than as a defensibility claim is analytically more useful. The evidence indicates that Fileverse occupies a specific position in the productivity-tooling space: encrypted Google Docs alternative for users who already operate onchain. CryptPad is the stronger choice for non-Web3 organizations needing a privacy-first replacement for Google Workspace without operational complexity; the [adoption signal in French ministries and German universities](https://blog.cryptpad.org/2023/12/14/CryptPad-2023-roadmap-recap/ "Source: CryptPad Blog, 2023-12-14") is direct evidence. Proton is the stronger choice for privacy-conscious individuals who value the [bundled Mail+Drive+Docs+VPN+Pass ecosystem and 100M+ accounts of scale](https://proton.me/blog/100-million-accounts "Source: Proton Blog, 2024-09-17"). ONLYOFFICE is the stronger procurement choice for enterprises whose top requirement is MS Office format compatibility plus self-hosting. Lit Protocol is the stronger choice when programmable conditional access (rather than static three-lock semantics) is the binding requirement; Lit is a candidate integration partner rather than a substitute. Dune is the stronger tool for deep historical onchain analysis and shared community dashboards.

Fileverse's distinct position is the intersection of three axes that no other listed peer covers simultaneously: E2EE-by-default productivity, content-addressed multi-provider persistence with onchain registry, and onchain-aware spreadsheet primitives. Whether that intersection corresponds to a sufficient addressable user base to support a self-sustaining operator is the open question the economic analysis engages.

### 5.5 Skiff as a structural reference point

Skiff warrants specific treatment because its trajectory is the closest commercial precedent. Skiff offered E2EE office, [opt-in IPFS pinning](https://skiff.com/blog/skiff-ipfs "Source: Wayback Machine — Skiff Blog, 2022-11-15"), [wallet-based authentication via MetaMask and Brave Wallet](https://skiff.com/blog/web3 "Source: Wayback Machine — Skiff Blog, retrieved 2026-04-27"), [USD 14.2 million in venture funding from Sequoia and others](https://techcrunch.com/2022/03/24/skiff-seed-round/ "Source: TechCrunch, 2022-03-24"), and Notion-comparable editing UX. Despite these advantages, the product was [acquihired by Notion in February 2024 and shut down on 2024-08-09](https://cyberinsider.com/skiff-shutting-down-alternatives-to-skiff-mail/ "Source: CyberInsider, 2024-02-12"). Two readings of this episode are coherent. The first is that Skiff's failure validates Fileverse's bet that decentralization-by-default — IPFS pinning as the default rather than opt-in, plus AGPL licensing — is the structural answer to the acquihire-then-sunset risk. Under that reading, Skiff is a cautionary precedent and Fileverse's design corrects the failure mode. The second reading is that Skiff's commercial death indicates the addressable market for E2EE+Web3 productivity may not yet support a venture-scale business; under that scenario, Fileverse's path may require a non-venture funding model (grants, foundation structure, commercial-license carve-out, or eventually a token). The two readings are not mutually exclusive; both can be true simultaneously, and the report treats both as live possibilities.

## 6. Economic & Game-Theoretic Properties

### 6.1 Dual-economy framing

Fileverse has no disclosed native token, no published fee schedule, and no announced venture round as of [the project's current public presence](https://fileverse.io "Source: Fileverse, retrieved 2026-04-27"). Standard tokenomics framing — bonding curves, staking yield, governance distribution — has no purchase here. The economic primitives that do exist are: UCAN-gated quotas on pinning, gas costs for the onchain content registry on Ethereum mainnet and Gnosis Chain, the cost-of-goods structure inherited from [Pinata](https://www.pinata.cloud/pricing "Source: Pinata, retrieved 2026-04-27"), [Filebase](https://filebase.com/pricing "Source: Filebase, retrieved 2026-04-27"), and [Web3.Storage / Storacha](https://blog.storacha.network/web3-storage-rebrands-to-storacha-network/ "Source: Storacha, 2024"), AGPL-3.0 licensing in the absence of disclosed funding, and the strategic choices facing self-hosters versus Fileverse-hosted users.

We therefore treat the system as a **dual-economy operation**: a free public good funded by an undisclosed mix of grants, founder equity, and (potentially) a future paid tier, layered with metered services (UCAN quotas, paymaster credits, premium tier) that constitute the only legible cash-flow path. This framing draws on [Rochet & Tirole's two-sided platform pricing](https://www.jstor.org/stable/40005188 "Rochet & Tirole, JEEA 2003"), in which one side (here, end-users) is subsidized by the other (here, premium tier or grants), and on [the local-first software essay](https://www.inkandswitch.com/local-first/ "Kleppmann et al., Ink & Switch 2019") which identifies the structural tension that local-first eliminates cloud lock-in but also eliminates cloud's revenue mechanism.

### 6.2 Participation incentive equilibria

The incentive loop has four distinguishable participants: end-users (U), the Fileverse operator (F), pinning providers (P_i ∈ {Pinata, Filebase, Storacha}), and self-hosters (S). Per-document monthly cost to F at a 1 MB midpoint with two collaborators editing 8 hours/week is approximately $0.01, dominated by bandwidth not storage. The cost is small per-document but unbounded in aggregate; cost per user scales with document count and re-fetch frequency, not seat count, which is the inverse of [Google Workspace's $6/seat/month structure](https://workspace.google.com/pricing.html "Source: Google, retrieved 2026-04-27").

In the symmetric equilibrium where all participants follow protocol intent, F bundles pinning costs into a free tier capped by quota, with overflow to a paid tier. Estimated break-even with 1,000 paying users at $5/month (a CryptPad-comparable tier where [CryptPad's lowest paid plan is €5/month](https://cryptpad.org/pricing/ "Source: CryptPad, retrieved 2026-04-27")) yields $5,000/month against roughly $2,000–$3,000/month in pinning at modest scale. That is feasible for infrastructure but does not cover engineering. F is therefore implicitly subsidized — by grants, founder equity, or a future raise — until a token or paid tier emerges.

The asymmetric scenario is more troubling. Early participation is **not** a dominant strategy in the standard tokenomic sense: there is no airdrop signal, no early-staker yield, no fee discount on accumulated activity. The dominant strategy for a rational, non-ideological user is wait-and-see — use the free tier without reciprocating, defect to CryptPad if Fileverse meters more aggressively, defect to a Notion-with-acquired-Skiff-tech feature if a Web2 incumbent ships an E2EE option. Early participation is dominant only for users who place positive utility on (a) ideological alignment with self-sovereignty, or (b) the option value of a future token retroactively rewarding usage — exactly the incentive Fileverse has not committed to. The architecture is sound; the participation incentive is weak.

### 6.3 Equilibrium boundaries

We can state explicit equilibrium conditions. The Fileverse-as-gateway equilibrium holds when (1) per-user revenue × paying-share ≥ per-user cost × total active users (i.e., F is cash-flow positive or grant-covered); (2) self-hosting share *q* < ~25% (above which F's gateway loses pricing power against pinning vendors and cannot negotiate enterprise discounts — [Filebase's enterprise tier discounts begin at 10 TB committed monthly](https://filebase.com/pricing/ "Source: Filebase, retrieved 2026-04-27")); (3) HHI of F's pinning spend across providers stays below ~5,000 (no single provider carrying >70% of spend); and (4) free-tier abuse stays below quota envelopes that force traffic onto the most expensive provider.

Equilibrium collapses when aggregate free-tier abuse pushes per-user cost above per-user revenue × paying-share for more than two quarters with no grant injection; when any single provider degrades or sunsets ([precedent: Storacha's 2024 free-tier wind-down](https://blog.storacha.network/web3-storage-rebrands-to-storacha-network/ "Source: Storacha, 2024")); when self-hosting share exceeds ~40%; or when a larger E2EE incumbent ([Proton, with Sheets launched in December 2025](https://www.macrumors.com/2025/12/04/proton-sheets-launches-encrypted-spreadsheet/ "Source: MacRumors, 2025-12-04")) prices a comparable feature at <$5/month and absorbs the privacy-conscious segment. The equilibrium is conditional and fragile: it requires sustained subsidy or a paid-tier launch within roughly 12–24 months under reasonable cost projections.

### 6.4 Unit economics scenarios

Three scenarios bound the analysis. In a **low scenario** with 25,000 registered users and 2% paid conversion, monthly recurring revenue is roughly $3,700 against infra costs of roughly $3,150 — covering infra but not engineering, implying grant or founder-equity subsidy. In a **medium scenario** with 200,000 registered and 4% conversion, MRR rises to roughly $70,400 against $60,000 in infra; gross margin around 15–30% blended, but engineering of $120K/month fully loaded for a 6–8 person team implies F still needs supplementary revenue. In a **scale scenario** with 1.5 million registered and 6% conversion, MRR reaches roughly $918K with infra COGS around $982K pre-discount, falling to roughly $550K at committed-volume rates (>10 TB triggers Filebase enterprise discount per [Filebase pricing](https://filebase.com/pricing/ "Source: Filebase, retrieved 2026-04-27")). Gross margin approaches 40% post-discount; the project becomes self-sustaining at this scale, funding a 15–25 person team without a token or VC layer.

The arithmetic shows the model is viable only at >100,000 paid users, equivalent to roughly 1.5M registered. Below that, Fileverse is structurally subsidy-dependent. [CryptPad reached approximately 5,000 paying users at ~280K registered after a decade](https://blog.cryptpad.org/2024/06/12/Onwards-and-upwards/ "Source: CryptPad Blog, 2024-06-12"); Fileverse would need to either grow faster than that or accept a long subsidy horizon.

### 6.5 Fee-structure corridor

Comparators set the pricing band: [CryptPad](https://cryptpad.org/pricing/ "Source: CryptPad, retrieved 2026-04-27") charges €5/month for 20 GB and €15/month for 1 TB; [Proton Drive bundling Docs and Sheets](https://proton.me/drive/pricing "Source: Proton, retrieved 2026-04-27") starts at $2.99/month and runs to $9.99/month for 1 TB Unlimited; [Anytype's Builder tier is $99/year](https://anytype.io/pricing "Source: Anytype Pricing, retrieved 2026-04-27") with $9.99/month equivalent; [Standard Notes Productivity is $90/year](https://standardnotes.com/plans "Source: Standard Notes, retrieved 2026-04-27"). Median paid-tier price is $5–$10/month for 100 GB–1 TB. Fileverse's price defection threshold against CryptPad is roughly $7/month (above which CryptPad's cheaper, longer-running offering wins); against Proton's bundled $9.99 it is roughly $10/month (above which the Proton bundle wins on dollar utility). Fileverse therefore has roughly a $5–$8 price corridor in which to operate.

A plausible structure within that corridor: free tier (1 GB pin, 5 GB egress, wallet-bound or vOPRF with rate-limit), Pro tier ($6/month, 50 GB pin and 200 GB egress), and an Onchain Premium tier ($20/month, including paymaster credits and ENS-based access policies) where Fileverse has a clear advantage over Proton/CryptPad since neither competes on wallet-native primitives. None of this is announced; it is a constraint analysis, not a proposed roadmap.

### 6.6 Attack and gaming surface

We highlight five vectors with quantified impact. **UCAN Quota Sybil:** if vOPRF-ID accounts can be created without economic cost, an attacker spawns N accounts to multiply free quota. At 1,000 sybil accounts and 100 MB pinned + 1 GB egress per account, this is roughly $150/month direct cost dumped on F at [Pinata's $0.15/GB egress](https://www.pinata.cloud/pricing "Source: Pinata, retrieved 2026-04-27"). At 10,000 sybils — achievable via cheap headless-browser farms — costs reach $6,500/month, which exceeds the low scenario's MRR of $3,700. Wallet-based authentication binds an account to a funded address and partially mitigates; vOPRF-only accounts do not. **Pinning Provider Cartel:** if two of three providers coordinate or one absorbs another, F's bargaining position degrades. The Storacha repositioning increased the cheapest fallback's price from $0/month to $10/month-minimum plus $0.05/GB; a 30% rate hike from one provider with concurrent capacity reduction at another forces 70% of volume onto the priciest path, a 40–60% COGS increase. **Link Lock Leakage:** as quantified above, a 1% per-link leakage rate on documents with average three shares yields roughly 3% silent confidentiality breach across the corpus. **Stateless Relay Free-Riding:** a non-Fileverse application using the relay as a free encrypted CRDT carrier costs F approximately $23/month per concurrent steady stream at [AWS data-transfer rates](https://aws.amazon.com/ec2/pricing/on-demand/#Data_Transfer "Source: AWS, retrieved 2026-04-27"); 100 such free-riders dump $2,300/month of unfunded bandwidth. **Onchain Registry Gas Griefing:** if F sponsors writes via paymaster, a 10× gas spike (30→300 gwei) raises per-write cost from $4.50 to $45; 100 Onchain Premium users at 100 sponsored writes/month exposes F to roughly $440K/month of unfunded gas during a sustained spike unless dynamic gas-cap throttling is in place.

### 6.7 Failure modes

Six failure modes are documented. **Pinning provider cascade failure** at medium-scenario volume puts roughly 170 TB at risk; emergency re-pinning costs roughly $850K one-time at Pinata-comparable emergency vendor pricing. **Triple-lock recovery path compromise** via Link Lock leakage produces silent confidentiality breaches whose reputational impact is asymmetric — comparable to the [Skiff sunset's impact on its category](https://www.theverge.com/24039915/skiff-shutting-down-notion-acquired "Source: The Verge, 2024-02-12"). **Sybil free-tier exhaustion** can exceed low-scenario MRR. **Self-host defection at scale**: 40% defection at scale-scenario volume drops MRR from $918K to $612K with engineering costs unchanged, compressing margin from 40% to 20%. **Onchain registry gas-cost inflation** during congestion windows drains paymaster faster than provisioned. **Audit gap on cryptographic library** is a universal-impact failure mode if a vulnerability in the lock-wrapping path is discovered before audit completion; recovery requires re-key plus re-pin of the entire corpus.

### 6.8 Capital efficiency under no-token framing

Under conditions where Fileverse never launches a token, capital efficiency is bounded by the rate at which paid-tier conversion grows relative to free-tier infrastructure burden. The mechanism's principal capital-efficiency advantage is that COGS scales with document count and bandwidth, not seat count — so a long-tail of low-activity users imposes lower marginal cost than a similarly sized seat-licensed product. The mechanism's principal capital-efficiency disadvantage is that it has no built-in mechanism to convert free users to paid users (unlike Proton's bundle wedge, which uses Mail and VPN to drive Drive/Docs adoption); Fileverse must invent its conversion funnel from scratch.

Under conditions where Fileverse does eventually launch a token, classical mechanism design (token as quota voucher, governance share, or retroactive contributor reward) becomes available. Quadratic-funding-style mechanisms drawing on [Buterin, Hitzig, and Weyl's design for funding public goods](https://arxiv.org/abs/1809.06421 "Buterin, Hitzig, Weyl, arXiv:1809.06421, 2018") could in principle finance Fileverse infra if a token or matched-grant program were introduced. Quadratic funding, however, requires identity resistance to sybil attacks, which Fileverse's vOPRF-ID partially provides but has not been formally evaluated for that use.

### 6.9 What the economics imply for the architecture

The economic constraints flow back into architectural decisions. The self-hosting threshold (the project should target self-hosting share in the 5–25% band) suggests Fileverse needs network-effect features unavailable to forks: paymaster credits whose keys are operator-controlled, cross-portal Waku discovery that has utility only at network scale, and a commercial-license carve-out for organizations seeking SLA plus indemnification beyond AGPL terms (the [MongoDB SSPL → commercial pattern](https://www.mongodb.com/legal/licensing/server-side-public-license "Source: MongoDB, retrieved 2026-04-27") is the precedent). None of these violate AGPL; all create gradient between fork and Fileverse-hosted experience.

The registry-chain default (Gnosis preferable to Ethereum mainnet) is a direct implication of the gas-griefing vector and the paymaster-exposure analysis. The tier-by-attestation quota policy (full quota for wallet-bound or domain-attested accounts, reduced quota for vOPRF-only with proof-of-personhood challenge) is a direct implication of the sybil-exhaustion vector. The audit-first sequencing on `@fileverse/crypto` is a direct implication of [Anderson's economics-of-security analysis](https://www.acsac.org/2001/papers/110.pdf "Anderson, ACSAC 2001"), which predicts under-investment in audits as a documented equilibrium for free-tier security software.

The evidence indicates the economic constraints are not separate from the architecture; they are constraints on which architectural choices remain viable as the user base scales. The dual-economy framing requires that the architecture supports both modes — public good and metered services — without compromising either. The current design supports both, but the gradient between them depends on choices not yet committed in public materials.

## 7. Open Questions & What Would Change Our View

### 7.1 Bear / Base / Bull cases

#### Bear case (≈200 words)

Under a bear scenario, the cryptographic-library audit on `@fileverse/crypto` reveals a vulnerability in the lock-wrapping path that compromises files at rest universally. Concurrent with that, [Storacha-style repositioning](https://blog.storacha.network/web3-storage-rebrands-to-storacha-network/ "Source: Storacha, 2024") cascades through the pinning vendor layer: Pinata raises prices materially or one provider exits, compressing F's COGS structure and exposing the multi-provider redundancy as a thinner safety net than advertised. A publicized confidentiality breach traceable to Link Lock URL-fragment leakage damages reputation in a way comparable to the [Skiff sunset's impact on its category](https://www.theverge.com/24039915/skiff-shutting-down-notion-acquired "Source: The Verge, 2024-02-12"). Sybil attacks on vOPRF-ID accounts dump unfunded cost on F; without a paid tier or grant injection, the gateway business becomes unsustainable. Self-hosting share rises past 40% as sophisticated organizations fork the AGPL codebase, taking the revenue base while leaving Fileverse-the-operator with the maintenance burden. **Assumptions:** audit reveals high-severity finding; one of three pinning providers exits or repositions adversely; sybil mitigation is not deployed before scale; no paid tier launches within 18 months. Under this scenario, Fileverse trajectory resembles Skiff's — architecturally interesting, commercially unviable.

#### Base case (≈200 words)

Under the base scenario, the [Dedalo audit on the collaboration-server](https://github.com/fileverse/collaboration-server "Source: Fileverse collaboration-server README, retrieved 2026-04-27") completes without high-severity findings; an independent audit on `@fileverse/crypto` follows within 6–12 months. Fileverse switches the registry default from Ethereum mainnet to Gnosis Chain, eliminating the gas-friction tax on document writes. A tier-by-attestation quota policy reduces sybil exposure to manageable levels. A paid tier launches in the $5–$10 corridor, achieving roughly 3,000 paying users within 12–18 months — covering infra COGS but not engineering, which remains grant-funded or founder-subsidized. Self-hosting share stabilizes in the 5–25% band. Conference-portal traction continues to compound as DAO operations and Web3-native research groups adopt dDocs and dSheets for non-public artifacts. **Assumptions:** audits complete on schedule; Gnosis default ships; sybil quota policy ships; paid-tier launches; no major pinning provider exits. Under this scenario, Fileverse becomes a sustainable but small operator, comparable in scale to CryptPad's current footprint, with a differentiated position in the Web3-native productivity niche but without crossing the threshold of mainstream adoption.

#### Bull case (≈200 words)

Under the bull scenario, the audit pipeline completes cleanly and Fileverse establishes a credible third-party-validated cryptographic posture. The Onchain Premium tier achieves disproportionate uptake among DAO operations and DeFi research teams — segments where the willingness to pay for paymaster credits, ENS-based access, and dSheets onchain-write quota is high. dSheets becomes a default tool in the analyst stack, partly displacing [Dune Analytics' $419–$833+/month per-seat tiers](https://dune.com/pricing "Source: Dune Pricing, retrieved 2026-04-27") for live-state operational workflows where Dune's read-only SQL is overkill. [Filecoin cold-storage deals add a fourth pinning tier](https://filecoin.io "Source: Filecoin, retrieved 2026-04-27") and remove cascade-failure risk. A grant-plus-commercial-license model funds engineering through 100,000+ paid users without requiring a token launch. Self-hosting share remains in the 5–25% band as network-effect features (paymaster, cross-portal discovery) preserve the gateway's value relative to forks. **Assumptions:** dSheets achieves a category-defining wedge; Onchain Premium conversion exceeds 25%; no major incident; multi-year grant pipeline secures engineering subsidy. Under this scenario, Fileverse becomes the canonical encrypted-and-onchain productivity surface for Web3-native organizations, comparable in influence to Safe's role in multisig.

### 7.2 What would change our view

The following are observable conditions that would meaningfully revise the report's conclusions. Each is falsifiable.

- **If Fileverse publishes evidence of self-sustaining unit economics at ≤30,000 paying users — for example through commercial-license revenue, large multi-year grants, or paid-tier conversion materially exceeding the [CryptPad analog of ~2% of registered users](https://blog.cryptpad.org/2024/06/12/Onwards-and-upwards/ "Source: CryptPad Blog, 2024-06-12") — the subsidy-dependent thesis in §6 is meaningfully revised.** The sustaining threshold of roughly 100,000 paying users (cross-referenced in Key Finding 2) becomes loose if the price ceiling is higher than $20/month or if a non-subscription revenue stream (commercial license, grants, treasury yield) exceeds $1M/year on a stable basis.

- **If the [Dedalo audit on the collaboration-server](https://github.com/fileverse/collaboration-server "Source: Fileverse collaboration-server README, retrieved 2026-04-27") publishes high-severity findings on the encrypted-update path, or if an independent audit on `@fileverse/crypto` reveals weaknesses in the lock-wrapping construction, the production-readiness assessment in Key Finding 5 worsens materially.** A clean audit completion strengthens the assessment symmetrically.

- **If Fileverse migrates the registry-default to Gnosis Chain (observable as the default `X-Chain-Id` header value emitted by [`fileverse-storage-v2`](https://github.com/fileverse/fileverse-storage-v2 "Source: Fileverse fileverse-storage-v2 README, retrieved 2026-04-27") and the default chain selector in dDocs and dSheets UIs), Key Finding 4 is resolved and the gas-friction observation no longer applies.** Continued mainnet default twelve months after this report would strengthen the friction critique.

- **If Fileverse ships a server-mediated key handshake or time-bounded Link Lock with on-chain expiry, replacing URL-fragment-based link sharing, the highest-severity attack vector identified in Key Finding 3 is mitigated and our view of the access-control posture revises upward.** Continued URL-fragment-only link sharing eighteen months after this report would strengthen the Link Lock critique.

- **If a major pinning provider (Pinata, Filebase, or Storacha) exits or undergoes Skiff-style acquihire-and-sunset, and Fileverse has not deployed a fourth fallback tier (own IPFS cluster or [Filecoin cold-storage deals](https://filecoin.io "Source: Filecoin, retrieved 2026-04-27")), the cascade-failure mode in §6.7 becomes the dominant risk and our view of operational robustness worsens.** Conversely, deployment of a fourth tier before such an event would strengthen the operational-robustness assessment.

- **If a credible E2EE-by-default productivity competitor (most likely [Proton via the Sheets launch trajectory](https://www.macrumors.com/2025/12/04/proton-sheets-launches-encrypted-spreadsheet/ "Source: MacRumors, 2025-12-04") plus a future onchain integration, or Notion deploying acquired-Skiff IPFS technology) prices a comparable feature at <$5/month and absorbs the privacy-conscious segment via existing distribution, the equilibrium-collapse condition in §6.3 triggers and the bear case becomes the central scenario.** Absence of such a competitor twelve months after this report strengthens the base case.

- **If Fileverse announces a token (as quota voucher, governance share, or retroactive contributor reward), the no-token framing in §6.1 is invalidated and the report would need re-scoping toward classical tokenomics analysis.** Continued no-token disclosure twelve months after this report strengthens the dual-economy framing as the appropriate model.

## 8. Outlook & Scenarios

The forward-looking analysis is framed conditionally throughout. No prescriptive recommendations are issued.

Under conditions where Fileverse executes the base case in §7.1, the project occupies a stable but specific position in the productivity-tooling space: encrypted Google Docs alternative for Web3-native organizations, with measurable but bounded penetration into adjacent segments. Conference portals continue to compound as a flagship use case; DAO operations and DeFi research teams adopt dDocs and dSheets at higher rates than mainstream knowledge workers; the paid-tier conversion settles in the 2–4% range comparable to [CryptPad's published metrics](https://blog.cryptpad.org/2024/06/12/Onwards-and-upwards/ "Source: CryptPad Blog, 2024-06-12"). The audit gap closes within 6–12 months of this report. Self-hosting share stabilizes in the 5–25% band. Under this trajectory, Fileverse's revenue footprint at the medium-scenario level (roughly $844K ARR per the §6.4 modeling) covers infra and a fraction of engineering; supplementary funding from grants, commercial-license carve-outs, or eventual token launch closes the residual gap. The project survives without crossing into mainstream adoption, comparable in trajectory to CryptPad's twelve-year arc but with onchain composability as a distinct capability.

Under conditions where the bull case obtains, dSheets emerges as a category-defining tool for live-state operational workflows in the Web3 stack, partly displacing read-only analytics tools for use cases where transaction submission is the binding requirement. Under those conditions, Fileverse's growth trajectory accelerates beyond CryptPad's analog, the Onchain Premium tier becomes the primary revenue line, and the project crosses the self-sustaining threshold at roughly the 100,000-paying-user mark identified in §6.4. A grant-plus-commercial-license model funds engineering without a token launch. The pinning vendor layer adds a Filecoin cold-storage tier and removes cascade-failure risk. Under these conditions, Fileverse's influence on the Web3-native productivity surface becomes structurally analogous to Safe's role in multisig — a default infrastructure layer rather than a competing application.

Under conditions where the bear case obtains, an audit finding, a pinning vendor exit, a Link Lock leakage incident, or an incumbent feature launch each individually represent path-dependent triggers that compress the timeline available to reach self-sustaining economics. If two or more of these conditions occur within a 12-month window, the equilibrium-collapse boundary identified in §6.3 is breached and Fileverse's trajectory increasingly resembles Skiff's. Under those conditions, the AGPL-3.0 license becomes load-bearing in a different way: the codebase survives the operating entity, and self-hosters and forks carry forward the technical artifacts even if the original gateway operation winds down. The walk-away tooling remains functional regardless of operator status, so end-user data does not become hostage to the operator's commercial state.

A separate dimension along which the outlook varies is the disclosure path. Under conditions where Fileverse adopts a Swiss non-profit foundation structure (the [Proton precedent of June 2024](https://proton.me/blog/proton-non-profit-foundation "Source: Proton Blog, 2024-06-17") and the [Anytype Any Association precedent](https://blog.anytype.io/anytype-1-0/ "Source: Anytype Blog, retrieved 2026-04-27")), the funding legibility critique in §5.3 weakens substantially and the project's longevity expectation rises. Under conditions where Fileverse remains in an undisclosed funding posture, the legibility critique persists and procurement evaluations from larger organizations face an unresolved sustainability question. The two paths are not symmetric: foundation structure is a one-way commitment, and adoption of that structure constitutes strong evidence of mission alignment that survives founder turnover.

A third dimension is the integration surface. Under conditions where [Lit Protocol's threshold conditional decryption](https://developer.litprotocol.com/network/networks/mainnet "Source: Lit Developer Docs, retrieved 2026-04-27") becomes a Fileverse integration option for portals requiring programmable conditional access, the static three-lock model gains a complement that addresses use cases where Portal/Owner/Link locks are structurally insufficient (for example, attestation-gated reads conditional on an arbitrary onchain state). Under conditions where Fileverse remains lock-only, the project trades simplicity for limited expressive range and Lit-using competitors retain an advantage in conditional-access-heavy use cases.

A fourth dimension is the secsync-style envelope migration. Under conditions where Fileverse ships per-message Ed25519 signatures and explicit key-rotation snapshots alongside the legacy `encrypted_yjs_update` envelope, role-based access (editor versus viewer) becomes cryptographically enforceable and former collaborators lose write access on revocation. Under conditions where the legacy envelope persists, trust-equal collaboration remains the operating mode and the design does not generalize cleanly to "open team" semantics.

Across all scenarios, two observations persist. First, the architecture's persistence story (multi-provider IPFS pinning plus AGPL-licensed walk-away tooling plus onchain registry) is the most durable asset of the design and is robust to most failure modes that affect the operating entity. Even in the bear case, the persistence story survives in the form of forks, self-hosters, and end-user-controlled IPFS pins. Second, the access-control story (URL-fragment Link Locks, opaque-relay encrypted-update envelope, vOPRF-ID identity recovery gap) is the most fragile asset of the design and is the layer most in need of further engineering investment regardless of the macro scenario. The gap between the persistence-layer maturity and the access-control-layer maturity is the most internally informative observation about where the project's load-bearing engineering work remains.

The evidence indicates that the question of which scenario obtains is conditional on observable engineering and disclosure events over the next 12–24 months, most of which are tracked in §7.2. The data cutoff for this report is 2026-04-27; any subsequent commit, audit publication, pricing change, competitor launch, or funding disclosure can shift the assessment along the axes identified above.
